# Fuel Theft Prevention — Operational Guidelines

## Background
Long-haul fleet operations are particularly vulnerable to fuel theft, which typically occurs through:
- Siphoning at unsecured overnight stops
- Driver collusion at certain fuel stations (under-delivery in exchange for cash)
- "Phantom fills" — forged receipts for fuel never actually pumped
- Tampering with fuel-level sensors to mask gradual siphonage

## Prevention Measures

### Vehicle-Level
- Locking fuel caps with unique driver keys
- Fuel level sensors with anti-tampering seals; flag any sensor that goes offline
- GPS-linked fuel monitoring with instant alerts on sudden drops outside dispenser sessions
- Camera coverage of fuel filler areas where feasible

### Operational
- Driver fuel cards tied to specific vehicle plates; mismatches flagged automatically
- Fuel-card transactions cross-referenced with telematics location at the time of fill
- Random spot checks of physical fuel level versus expected level after long stops
- Approved overnight parking only at secured depots or designated truck stops

## Detection Signals
The strongest indicator of siphonage is a sudden drop of more than 5% fuel level over a 2-3 hour window while the vehicle is stationary, with no dispenser transaction recorded. Pair this with:
- **Location**: was the vehicle at a fuel station? a known unsecured stop?
- **Time of day**: siphonage is overwhelmingly nocturnal (00:00-04:00)
- **Recent driver history**: are there repeated incidents on the same driver's shifts?

A single isolated drop may indicate a sensor fault; a recurring pattern is theft.

## Escalation
Repeated unexplained fuel drops on the same vehicle or shift pattern require:
1. Driver interview
2. Sensor calibration check
3. GPS log audit of overnight stops
4. If theft is confirmed: formal incident report, route reassignment, and (for collusion cases) supplier audit at the relevant fuel station.
