# Electrical Fault Diagnosis

## Symptoms by Category

### Cranking & Starting
- **No crank, no lights** — dead battery, loose terminal, or main fuse failure.
- **No crank, lights dim** — battery is flat or very low; charging system also at fault if recently driven.
- **No crank, lights normal** — starter motor or solenoid failure. Tap the starter while assistant cranks; if it then starts, the solenoid contacts are worn.
- **Slow crank** — weak battery, corroded terminals, or starter motor brushes worn.

### Running
- **Vehicle dies at idle** — alternator output insufficient; the vehicle has been running off battery alone.
- **Intermittent ignition loss** — ignition switch, immobiliser, or main relay failing under heat.
- **Warning lights flickering** — ground strap loose or corroded, or alternator output unstable.

## Diagnostic Sequence
1. Measure battery voltage at rest (engine off, 10 minutes after last drive). Should be 12.4-12.8V. Below 12.2V indicates discharge or a failed cell.
2. Measure voltage while cranking. Should not drop below 9.6V — lower readings indicate a weak battery.
3. Measure voltage while engine runs at 2000 RPM. Should be 13.8-14.4V — this is the alternator output. Outside this range suggests alternator failure.
4. Clean battery terminals; check for corrosion (green or white residue around clamps). Corroded terminals are the cause of ~30% of "no crank" complaints.
5. Inspect the ground strap from engine block to chassis. Corroded or loose grounds cause a majority of "weird electrical" problems — flickering lights, erratic gauge readings, random fault codes.

## When to Replace vs. Repair
- **Battery** older than 4 years and showing low voltage: replace, do not attempt to recover.
- **Alternator** with output below 13.5V at 2000 RPM: replace.
- **Starter motor** with intermittent failures: rebuild or replace; do not attempt to "knock it" with a hammer as a long-term fix.
- **Wiring** with visible damage: full harness replacement is sometimes cheaper than splicing in the long run.
