# Breakdown Recovery Protocol

## Stage 1 — Driver Makes Safe
1. Activate hazard lights immediately.
2. Move the vehicle off the carriageway if possible. Even disabled, a vehicle can usually coast onto a hard shoulder or layby.
3. Set the parking brake. Place chocks if on any incline.
4. Deploy reflective triangles: 50m ahead and 50m behind for normal roads, 100m for highways.
5. Wear a high-visibility vest before exiting the cab.
6. Stand well clear of the carriageway — behind the safety barrier where one exists.
7. Call dispatch on the breakdown line. Provide: vehicle plate, GPS coordinates if known, milestone marker, road name, and the nature of the fault.

## Stage 2 — Dispatch Assesses
1. Confirm driver safety and the exact location.
2. Categorise the fault:
   - **Roadworthy with limitation** — drive at reduced speed to nearest depot or workshop (with dispatch concurrence). Examples: dashboard warning light without immediate symptoms; alternator failing but battery still charged.
   - **Not roadworthy** — recovery vehicle required. Examples: brake failure, overheating, suspension damage, blown tyre.
3. Cross-check the incident type against the vehicle's maintenance history. Vehicles with three breakdowns in 90 days require an unscheduled inspection regardless of root cause.

## Stage 3 — Recovery Resolves
- Target on-road recovery time: 60 minutes urban, 90 minutes highway or rural.
- Tow to the nearest approved workshop unless the driver is medically incapacitated, in which case priority shifts to driver care.
- Log the incident in the fleet system with: cause, recovery time, downtime, repair cost. This feeds the breakdown analytics that flag vehicles needing proactive maintenance.

## Driver Welfare
Drivers stranded for more than 2 hours must be offered relief — water, food, and transport home if recovery extends past their duty hours. Drivers are not expected to remain with the vehicle if their shift ends and recovery is delayed; the recovery operator will secure the vehicle.

## Post-Incident
Within 24 hours of recovery, the driver must file a written breakdown report describing what happened, what they did, and how it was resolved. This report is reviewed at the weekly operations meeting and feeds both maintenance planning and driver training.
