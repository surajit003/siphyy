# Fuel Efficiency — Operational Best Practices

## Driver Behaviour (40-60% of efficiency variance)
- **Smooth acceleration** — aggressive acceleration burns 30% more fuel than gradual.
- **Anticipate stops** — coast to red lights rather than braking late.
- **Maintain steady highway speed** — use cruise control on flat highways where possible.
- **Avoid idling** — 10 minutes of idling consumes ~0.5L of fuel. Switch off the engine for any stop longer than one minute.
- **Top gear early** — keep RPM below 2000 in normal driving on flat ground.

## Vehicle Configuration
- **Tyre pressure** — 0.3 bar low equals roughly 3% efficiency loss. Check monthly.
- **Wheel alignment** — misalignment of 2-3 degrees equals 5-8% efficiency loss.
- **Air filter** — a clogged filter equals up to 10% efficiency loss. Replace per the scheduled interval.
- **Roof racks** — an empty roof rack costs 2-5% efficiency on the highway. Remove when not in use.

## Loading
- Distribute load evenly across the chassis. Heavy rear loading creates aerodynamic drag.
- Do not carry unnecessary weight. 100kg of extra mass equals 1-2% efficiency loss.
- Loose loads also affect efficiency through unstable handling and additional braking.

## Route Selection
- Highway is more efficient than urban — steady speed, fewer stops.
- Flat routes outperform hilly ones by 10-15% for the same distance.
- Avoid known congestion corridors at peak times even if the route is slightly longer.

## Monitoring
The fleet system flags any vehicle with fuel economy more than 15% worse than its rolling 30-day baseline. Vehicles in this state should be diagnosed within a week — most cases are recoverable with simple maintenance (tyre pressure, air filter, alignment). Persistent decay after these checks points to deeper issues: failing injectors, drivetrain drag, or driver behaviour requiring coaching.
